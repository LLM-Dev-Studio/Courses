# Myths, Risks, and Reality Checks

Every major technology shift brings hype, fear, and confusion. AI is no different. Before JJ's teams adopt AI at scale, it is important to separate what is true from what is marketing noise.

> [!NOTE]
>
> **What you'll learn:**
> - The most common myths about AI in operations and customer service
> - The real risks that matter for a safety-focused business
> - When AI is useful and when it is not the right tool
> - How to make practical, low-risk AI decisions with a clear business case

## Myth 1: "AI Will Replace Our Teams"

This is one of the most common concerns. In real business settings, AI usually changes **how** people work rather than removing the need for people.

**Reality:** AI handles repetitive first-pass work (drafting, summarising, formatting, sorting information). People still handle judgement, customer conversations, safety decisions, escalations, and final accountability.

At JJ's, AI can support teams by reducing admin load, but it does not replace frontline experience, compliance obligations, or operational judgement.

> [!TIP]
>
> Position AI internally as "support for the repetitive parts" rather than "replacement for people." Adoption is stronger when teams see AI as a tool that helps them do better work.

## Myth 2: "AI Is Too Expensive to Start"

Many teams assume AI requires a large transformation program on day one.

**Reality:** Start small. Most teams begin with low-cost or included tools and focus on one practical workflow at a time.

Examples of low-risk starting points:
- Drafting internal communications and service updates
- Summarising long documents and meeting notes
- Creating first-pass SOP wording or toolbox talk outlines
- Preparing customer response templates for human review

The key question is not "how advanced is the tool?" but "does this save time, improve consistency, or reduce risk in a measurable way?"

## Myth 3: "AI Is Only for Big Corporates"

Large organisations may have bigger budgets, but they also have slower change cycles.

**Reality:** Practical AI adoption often moves faster in operational businesses that can test quickly and learn quickly.

JJ's teams can pilot one process, measure impact, improve the approach, then scale where it works. That is often more effective than trying to design a perfect enterprise rollout upfront.

## Myth 4: "AI Is Always Right"

This is the most dangerous myth.

AI can produce polished, confident output that is incomplete, inaccurate, or unsuitable for your context.

**Reality:** AI output is a draft, not a decision.

For JJ's workflows, always verify:
- Safety-related statements
- Compliance and legal references
- Dates, numbers, pricing, and incident details
- Customer-specific or site-specific facts

> [!WARNING]
>
> Never publish or action AI output in safety-critical or compliance-critical workflows without human review.

## Myth 5: "You Need to Be Technical to Use AI"

Modern AI tools are designed for plain-language instructions.

**Reality:** You do not need to be a developer to get value. You do need to understand:
- What good prompts look like
- Which tasks are suitable for AI
- Where human checks are mandatory
- How to protect company and customer data

These are practical operational skills, not specialist engineering skills.

## When AI Is NOT the Right Tool

Do not use AI when:
- A task requires guaranteed factual precision and no review time is available
- The workflow involves sensitive decisions that must remain human-led
- Data handling requirements are unclear
- The use case has no clear business or operational benefit

If the objective, owner, and success measure are not clear, pause and scope the problem first.

## Practical JJ's Decision Test

Before adopting AI for any workflow, ask:
1. What exact business problem are we solving?
2. What risk level does this workflow carry?
3. What must always be human-reviewed?
4. How will we measure value (time saved, quality improved, errors reduced)?
5. Who owns this process if something goes wrong?

If you cannot answer these clearly, do not scale yet.

## Key Takeaway

AI is powerful when applied to the right problem with the right controls. The goal is not to "use AI everywhere." The goal is to use AI where it improves speed, consistency, and quality while keeping people accountable for outcomes.
